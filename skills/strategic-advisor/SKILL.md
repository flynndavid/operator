# Strategic Advisor

_Help the founder think about the business from 30,000 feet — then bring it back to earth with a plan._

---

## When to Use This

- Client is at an inflection point (growing fast, stalling, or at a crossroads)
- Quarterly or annual strategic review
- New competitor enters the market
- Pricing feels wrong (too low, too high, too confusing)
- Client wants to add a revenue stream or enter a new market
- Cash flow is getting unpredictable
- The founder is asking "what should I focus on?"

---

## Market Positioning Analysis

### Where Do You Sit?

Help the client map their competitive landscape. Not a formal "Porter's Five Forces" exercise — a practical conversation.

**1. Who are the real competitors?**
Not every business in your category is a competitor. Your real competitors are the ones your specific customers considered before choosing you. Ask: "Who did your last 5 customers almost go with instead?"

**2. What are you competing on?**
Map the dimensions that matter to your customers:

| Dimension | You | Competitor A | Competitor B |
|-----------|-----|-------------|-------------|
| Price | | | |
| Speed/convenience | | | |
| Quality/craft | | | |
| Specialization | | | |
| Relationships/trust | | | |
| Experience/reputation | | | |

**3. Where's the gap?**
Look for underserved combinations. Maybe everyone in your market competes on price, and nobody competes on specialization + relationships. That's your opening.

**4. The "Only" Statement**
Fill in: "We are the ONLY [type of business] that [unique thing] for [specific audience]."
If you can't fill this in, your positioning needs work. If a competitor could say the same thing, it's not differentiated enough.

## Pricing Strategy

### The Value-Based Pricing Conversation

Most SMBs price based on costs + margin or "what competitors charge." Both are wrong for businesses that deliver real value.

**Step 1: What's it worth to the customer?**
Not what it costs you — what does the customer gain? If your event planning saves a corporate client 40 hours of internal time and eliminates the risk of a bad event, the value is far more than your hourly rate suggests.

**Step 2: Price anchoring**
What's the alternative? If they don't hire you, what do they do instead? (DIY, hire a competitor, don't do it at all.) Your price should feel like a good deal compared to the best alternative.

**Step 3: Packaging**
Three tiers almost always outperforms one price:
- **Entry:** Solves the core problem. Accessible. Gets people in the door.
- **Standard:** The one you want most people to buy. Best value for the most common need.
- **Premium:** For people who want the best and don't want to think about it. Higher margin, higher touch.

### Pricing Red Flags
- You haven't raised prices in over a year
- You win almost every deal (you're probably too cheap)
- You're busy but not profitable (pricing-volume mismatch)
- Customers never push back on price (they would've paid more)
- You discount frequently to close deals (price isn't anchored to value)

### When to Revisit
Review pricing at least every 6 months. Trigger an immediate review when:
- Costs increase meaningfully
- You add significant new capabilities
- Customer profile shifts
- A competitor makes a major pricing move

## Revenue Model Evaluation

### Is the Model Working?

| Question | Healthy | Concerning |
|----------|---------|------------|
| Can you predict next month's revenue? | Within 20% | No idea |
| What % is recurring vs. one-time? | Growing recurring | All project-based |
| Top customer concentration? | No single customer > 20% | One customer > 40% |
| Revenue per customer trending? | Stable or growing | Declining |
| Customer acquisition cost known? | Yes, and it's sustainable | No idea |

### Revenue Diversification Options

When a client is too dependent on one revenue stream:
- **Add a recurring component** to a project-based business (retainers, subscriptions, maintenance)
- **Productize expertise** (courses, templates, tools, guides)
- **Expand the offer** to adjacent needs (the catering company that also does event staffing)
- **Add a channel** (direct + wholesale, online + in-person, B2B + B2C)

Rule of thumb: don't diversify for its own sake. Diversify when concentration creates risk or when there's a natural extension your customers are already asking for.

## Partnership and Channel Strategy

### Finding the Right Partners

The best partnerships share customers but don't compete. Ask:
- What does my customer need before they need me? (That business is a partner.)
- What does my customer need after they use me? (Also a partner.)
- Who serves the same audience for a different need? (Also a partner.)

### Partnership Tiers

| Tier | What It Looks Like | Effort |
|------|-------------------|--------|
| **Referral** | You send each other customers. Informal, low commitment. | Low |
| **Co-marketing** | Joint events, shared content, cross-promotion. | Medium |
| **Bundled offering** | You package your services together for a combined offer. | Medium-High |
| **Strategic alliance** | Deep integration. Shared revenue. Joint go-to-market. | High |

Start at referral. Only move deeper when the value is proven and the relationship is solid.

### Channel Strategy for SMBs
- Direct (you sell it yourself) should be the foundation
- Referral partners before paid channels
- Marketplaces and platforms only when you've proven the offer works direct
- Don't build a channel you can't sustain with current resources

## Risk Assessment

### The SMB Risk Map

Run through these quarterly:

| Risk Category | Questions to Ask |
|---------------|-----------------|
| **Customer concentration** | Would losing your top customer hurt badly? Top 3? |
| **Key person dependency** | If the founder disappeared for a month, what breaks? |
| **Supplier/vendor risk** | Any single vendor that could take you down? |
| **Market shifts** | What's changing in your industry that could disrupt you in 12 months? |
| **Cash flow** | How many months of runway do you have if revenue dropped 30%? |
| **Regulatory** | Any compliance or legal changes on the horizon? |
| **Reputation** | One bad review, one social media incident — how resilient is your brand? |

### Mitigation Playbook
For each high-risk item, create a simple plan:
- **What's the risk?** (Specific scenario)
- **How likely?** (Low/Medium/High)
- **How bad?** (Annoying/Painful/Existential)
- **What reduces it?** (One concrete action)

Focus on the high-likelihood + high-impact quadrant. Don't waste energy on low-probability risks unless they're existential.

## Seasonal Planning and Cash Flow

### Cash Flow Forecasting (Simple Version)

Most SMBs don't need complex financial models. They need this:

| Month | Expected Revenue | Expected Expenses | Net | Cash on Hand |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|-----|-------------|
| [Month] | $ | $ | $ | $ |

Fill this out 6 months forward. Update monthly. The goal isn't precision — it's seeing trouble before it arrives.

### Seasonal Strategy
- **Identify your seasons:** When does revenue peak and dip? Plot the last 12 months.
- **Pre-season (before the peak):** Build capacity, hire temps, stock up, market harder
- **Peak season:** Execute, don't experiment. Capture demand, not create it.
- **Post-season:** Evaluate, plan, rest. Try new things when the stakes are lower.
- **Off-season:** This is when you build the machine. Systems, training, strategy, relationships.

### Cash Flow Rules of Thumb
- Keep 3 months of operating expenses in reserve (6 if your revenue is volatile)
- Invoice fast, collect faster. Every day of AR is your money sitting in someone else's account.
- Know your break-even number — the monthly revenue where you cover all costs. Keep it visible.
- Before any major expense: "What's the revenue impact, and when?"

## Growth vs. Sustainability Tradeoffs

### The Growth Question

Not every business should be growing right now. Help the client think clearly:

**Grow when:**
- Demand exceeds capacity and you're turning away good work
- You've found a repeatable, profitable model and want to scale it
- Market conditions favor moving fast (new market, weakened competitor)
- The founder has the energy and the team has the capability

**Consolidate when:**
- Quality is slipping because you're stretched thin
- Profit margins are declining as you grow (growth is eating itself)
- The founder is burning out
- You haven't systematized what's working yet (growing chaos, not a business)
- Cash reserves are thin

**Shrink when:**
- A revenue stream is unprofitable and unfixable
- A customer segment is draining resources disproportionately
- The market has shifted and the old model isn't coming back
- Less revenue but higher profit and better quality of life is the honest best path

### The Sustainability Checklist
Before any growth initiative, check:
- [ ] Can our systems handle 2x volume without breaking?
- [ ] Can our team handle the increased load?
- [ ] Do we have the cash to fund the growth period (growth usually costs before it pays)?
- [ ] Is the founder prepared for what "bigger" actually feels like day-to-day?
- [ ] Are we growing toward something specific, or just growing because it feels like we should?

If more than two boxes are unchecked, it's probably not time to push growth. Build the foundation first.
