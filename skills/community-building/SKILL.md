# Community Building

_Help the business build a real community — not a mailing list with a Slack channel._

---

## When to Use This

- Client wants to grow through word-of-mouth instead of (or alongside) paid acquisition
- Client has loyal customers but hasn't organized them into a community
- Client is entering a new market and needs to build trust fast
- Client's marketing feels transactional and they want it to feel relational
- Retention is a problem and community could be the fix

---

## Audience Mapping

Before building anything, figure out where your people already are.

### Discovery Questions

1. **Where do your best customers spend time?** (Not where you wish they were — where they actually are)
   - Online: specific subreddits, Facebook groups, Discord servers, forums, newsletters they read
   - Offline: events they attend, organizations they belong to, places they hang out

2. **Who do they already trust?** Local influencers, industry voices, adjacent businesses, community leaders

3. **What do they talk about when they're not buying from you?** This tells you what content and conversations to build around

4. **Where did your last 10 customers hear about you?** If you don't know, start asking. This is the single most important question for community strategy.

### Map Output
Create a simple table:

| Channel/Place | Our Audience There? | Our Presence Today | Opportunity |
|---------------|---------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| [e.g., Local Chamber events] | High | Low | Show up monthly |
| [e.g., r/[niche] subreddit] | Medium | None | Contribute, don't sell |

Pick the top 2-3 opportunities. Don't try to be everywhere.

## Community Engagement Playbook

### The 80/20 Rule of Community
- **80% give:** Help, educate, celebrate, connect people to each other
- **20% ask:** Promote, sell, request reviews, ask for referrals

If you flip this ratio, you don't have a community — you have a marketing channel that people will eventually tune out.

### Engagement Activities (Pick What Fits)

**Low effort, high consistency:**
- Reply to every review, comment, and mention personally
- Share customer wins publicly (with permission)
- Ask genuine questions in the spaces your audience frequents
- Send a personal note to new customers (not automated — actually personal)

**Medium effort, high impact:**
- Monthly spotlight on a customer, partner, or community member
- Host a casual monthly meetup (virtual or in-person, depending on the business)
- Create a simple referral program that rewards both sides
- Run a "community challenge" tied to what your business does

**Higher effort, compounding returns:**
- Launch a private group (Slack, Discord, Facebook, or in-person) for your best customers
- Host a quarterly event — workshop, panel, party, whatever fits
- Build a content series featuring community members
- Create an "advisory board" of 5-10 customers who give you input and feel invested

## Referral and Word-of-Mouth Programs

### Design Principles
- Make it easy to refer (one link, one message, no hoops)
- Reward the referrer AND the new customer (both sides of the relationship matter)
- Make the reward meaningful but not transactional — a heartfelt thank-you note can be more powerful than a 10% discount
- Track referrals so you can personally thank top referrers

### Simple Referral Framework

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Trigger:** | When does the ask happen? (After a great experience, at delivery, at a milestone) |
| **Ask:** | What exactly do you say? (Keep it natural — "Know anyone who'd love this?") |
| **Mechanism:** | How do they refer? (Link, card, introduction, review) |
| **Reward — Referrer:** | What do they get? (Credit, gift, recognition, early access) |
| **Reward — Referred:** | What does the new person get? (Welcome offer, special treatment) |
| **Follow-up:** | How do you close the loop? (Thank the referrer, let them know it worked) |

### Making Word-of-Mouth Happen
Word-of-mouth isn't random — it's triggered by moments worth talking about. Help the client identify and engineer those moments:
- Unexpected quality or attention to detail
- Going above and beyond in a visible way
- Creating an experience that's inherently shareable
- Solving a problem so well the customer can't help but tell someone

## Retention and Loyalty

### The Loyalty Ladder
Not all customers are equal. Help the client see and serve each tier:

1. **First-timers:** Just discovered you. Goal: make the first experience remarkable.
2. **Returners:** Came back a second time. Goal: learn their name and preferences.
3. **Regulars:** Consistent customers. Goal: make them feel like insiders.
4. **Advocates:** They send you business. Goal: recognize and reward them.
5. **Community Leaders:** They actively champion you. Goal: give them a seat at the table.

### Retention Actions by Tier

| Tier | Action |
|------|--------|
| First-timer → Returner | Personal follow-up after first purchase. Ask how it went. |
| Returner → Regular | Remember their preferences. Surprise them occasionally. |
| Regular → Advocate | Ask for feedback, act on it visibly. Invite to exclusive events. |
| Advocate → Leader | Advisory board invitation. Co-creation opportunities. Public recognition. |

## Community Health Metrics

Track these monthly:

| Metric | What It Tells You |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Active participation rate** | Are people actually engaging, or just subscribed? |
| **New member sources** | Where are new community members coming from? |
| **Member-to-member connections** | Are people connecting with each other, not just with you? |
| **Referral rate** | What % of new customers come from existing ones? |
| **Response to community events** | Are people showing up? Is attendance growing? |
| **Unsolicited content/reviews** | Are people talking about you without being prompted? |
| **Churn from community** | Are engaged community members leaving? (Red flag if yes) |

### Red Flags
- Community feels like a broadcast channel (you talk, they listen)
- Same 3-5 people doing all the engaging
- People join but don't participate
- The community only activates around discounts/deals

If you see these patterns, the community needs less marketing and more genuine connection. Go back to basics: be useful, be present, be human.

## Event Planning (Community Events)

### Event Types That Build Community

| Type | Best For | Effort |
|------|----------|--------|
| Casual hangout (coffee, happy hour) | Early community building | Low |
| Workshop / teach something | Establishing expertise | Medium |
| Customer showcase | Social proof + recognition | Medium |
| Partner collaboration event | Expanding reach | Medium |
| Annual gathering / celebration | Community identity | High |

### Event Checklist
1. **Purpose:** What's this event supposed to do for the community? (Not for your pipeline — for the community)
2. **Format:** Match the format to your audience. Don't do a webinar if your people want to meet in person.
3. **Promotion:** Let the community help spread the word. Give them a reason to invite someone.
4. **Experience:** One memorable moment > a packed agenda. Less is more.
5. **Follow-up:** Share photos, highlights, and thanks within 48 hours. Make people who couldn't attend wish they had.
6. **Capture:** What did you learn? What should you do differently? Who showed up that you should deepen a relationship with?
