# Brand Strategy

_Help the business figure out what it actually stands for — and make that count._

---

## When to Use This

- Client is starting out and needs to define their positioning
- Client is growing and their brand has gotten fuzzy or inconsistent
- Competitive pressure is increasing and they need to sharpen what makes them different
- Client is preparing for a rebrand, new market, or major pivot
- Regular brand health check (quarterly recommended)

---

## Brand Positioning Workshop

Walk the client through these questions — not as a form to fill out, but as a conversation. Push back on generic answers. "We provide great service" isn't positioning. "We're the only catering company in Austin that does farm-to-table for events under 50 people" is.

### The Core Questions

**1. Who do you serve?**
Not "everyone." The specific people who are your best, happiest customers. Describe them like you're describing a friend — what do they care about, what keeps them up at night, where do they hang out?

**2. What do you do for them?**
Not your service list. The transformation. Before and after. "We turn stressful corporate events into the thing people actually talk about afterwards."

**3. Why you?**
What do you have that a competitor doesn't? Be honest — if you can't answer this, that's the real problem to solve. Possibilities:
- Deep expertise in a specific niche
- A proprietary process or method
- Geographic or community lock-in
- Relationships that took years to build
- A founder story that creates genuine connection

**4. What would you never do?**
Knowing what you're NOT tells people what you ARE. The company that says "we never take more than 10 clients at a time" is saying something about quality without claiming to be "premium."

### Output: Positioning Statement
One sentence that passes the "would a stranger understand what makes this business different?" test.

Format: `We help [specific audience] [achieve specific outcome] by [what makes us different].`

## Brand Story Framework

Every business has a story. Most just haven't told it well. Help the client craft theirs.

### The Elements

1. **The Origin:** Why does this business exist? Not the business plan reason — the real reason. The frustration, the opportunity, the moment of "someone should do this."
2. **The Struggle:** What was hard about getting here? People connect with struggle, not smooth sailing.
3. **The Insight:** What did you figure out that others haven't? This is the intellectual heart of the brand.
4. **The Mission:** Where are you going? What does success look like for the people you serve?

### Story Format
Write three versions:
- **The Elevator (30 seconds):** Two sentences that make someone say "tell me more"
- **The About Page (2 minutes):** The full narrative with personality
- **The Campfire (5 minutes):** The version the founder tells over drinks — the real, unpolished, compelling version

## Moat Analysis

Help the client identify and strengthen their competitive moat.

### Moat Types for SMBs

| Moat Type | What It Looks Like | How to Strengthen |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Niche expertise** | You know more about this specific thing than anyone in the area | Publish, teach, get cited, build credentials |
| **Community lock-in** | Your customers know each other and form a network | Events, groups, referral programs, shared identity |
| **Switching costs** | Leaving you means losing something valuable | Deep integrations, accumulated history, custom workflows |
| **Local trust** | Years of showing up in the same community | Sponsorships, partnerships, local hiring, visible presence |
| **Founder brand** | People buy because of WHO you are | Speaking, content, personal story, thought leadership |
| **Process advantage** | You do something in a way nobody else can easily copy | Document it, name it, make it part of the pitch |

### Assessment
Rate each moat type 1-5 for the business. Identify the strongest one or two and build strategy around deepening them.

## Brand Voice Guide

Create a practical voice guide the agent uses for all client communications.

### Template

**We sound like:** [describe the vibe — e.g., "a knowledgeable friend who happens to be an expert"]
**We don't sound like:** [anti-patterns — e.g., "a corporate press release" or "a try-hard influencer"]

**Vocabulary:**
- Words we use: [list]
- Words we avoid: [list]

**Tone adjustments by context:**
- Customer support: [warmer, more patient]
- Marketing copy: [confident, specific]
- Social media: [casual, community-oriented]
- Proposals/pitches: [professional but still human]

**The Test:** Before sending anything, ask: "Would [client name] actually say this?" If not, rewrite it.

## Brand-Building Activity Plan

Help the client plan ongoing brand-building — not campaigns, but consistent presence.

### Monthly Brand Calendar

| Activity | Frequency | Purpose |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Customer spotlight / case study | Monthly | Social proof + relationship deepening |
| Community participation (events, forums, local groups) | Weekly | Visibility + trust |
| Educational content (blog, video, newsletter) | Bi-weekly | Authority + audience building |
| Partnership activation (co-marketing, joint events) | Monthly | Reach extension |
| Internal brand alignment (team brand training) | Quarterly | Consistency |

### Brand Health Signals

Track these — not as vanity metrics, but as relationship health indicators:

- **Repeat business rate:** Are customers coming back?
- **Referral source tracking:** How many new customers come from word-of-mouth?
- **Unprompted mentions:** Are people talking about you without being asked?
- **Response to price increases:** Do customers accept them or leave? (Trust indicator)
- **Quality of inbound inquiries:** Are the right people finding you?
- **Employee pride:** Does your team actively recommend working here?

Review brand health signals quarterly. If multiple signals decline simultaneously, something fundamental has shifted — don't just tweak marketing, dig into the root cause.
