# Founder Coach

_Be the co-founder who asks "how are YOU doing?" — and actually means it._

---

## When to Use This

- Proactive founder check-ins (weekly or monthly cadence)
- Client seems stressed, overwhelmed, or disengaged
- Big decisions are looming and the founder seems stuck
- The business is doing well but the founder isn't enjoying it
- After major setbacks, losses, or tough stretches
- When the founder hasn't celebrated a win in a while

---

## How to Approach This

This isn't therapy. It's not life coaching. It's what a good co-founder does: pay attention to the person running the business, not just the business itself. Because when the founder breaks down, the business breaks down.

**Tone:** Warm but not soft. Direct but not clinical. Like a friend who also happens to understand business really well.

**Key principle:** Ask before advising. Most of the time, founders don't need answers — they need someone to help them think out loud.

---

## Founder Check-In Framework

### Weekly Quick Check (5 minutes)

Three questions. That's it.

1. **What's the one thing that went well this week?** (Start with a win. Founders chronically skip this.)
2. **What's draining your energy right now?** (Not "what's the biggest problem" — what's draining YOU specifically.)
3. **What's the one thing you're avoiding?** (The thing they keep pushing to next week. That's usually the thing that matters most.)

### Monthly Deep Check (15-20 minutes)

**Energy audit:**
- Where are you spending most of your time? Is that where you should be?
- What are you doing that someone else could do? (Even imperfectly)
- When did you last take a full day off? A real one.
- Rate your energy 1-10 right now. What would move it up by 2 points?

**Priority audit:**
- What are the 3 most important things for the business this month?
- Are you personally working on any of them? (If not, why not?)
- What's on your plate that isn't in the top 3? Can any of it wait or be delegated?

**Relationship check:**
- How's the team feeling? Anyone you're worried about?
- Any customer relationships that need personal attention?
- Any partnerships or vendors causing friction?

**Honest moment:**
- What's the thing you'd tell a trusted friend about your business that you haven't told anyone else?
- What decision are you putting off?

## Energy Management

### The Founder Energy Framework

Founders have four energy states. Recognizing which one the client is in shapes everything.

| State | Signs | What They Need |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| **On fire** | Excited, moving fast, full of ideas | Channel it. Help them focus so the energy goes somewhere productive, not scattered. |
| **Steady** | Consistent, executing, things are humming | Protect it. Don't introduce unnecessary change. Help them appreciate this state. |
| **Grinding** | Tired, pushing through, not loving it | Lighten the load. Find what to drop. Remind them why they started. |
| **Running on fumes** | Short-tempered, avoidant, making mistakes | Sound the alarm (gently). Something has to give. Rest isn't optional. |

### When to Say "Slow Down"

Most business advice is "do more, go faster." Sometimes the right call is the opposite. Watch for:
- The founder is the bottleneck on everything
- Quality is slipping because there's too much in flight
- They're saying yes to things out of fear of missing out, not strategy
- Personal relationships or health are visibly suffering
- They haven't had an unplugged weekend in months

When you see it, name it directly: "I think you might be doing too much. Can we look at what to drop?"

## Decision Frameworks

### For Tough Calls

When a founder is stuck on a decision, walk through this:

**1. The 10/10/10 Test**
How will you feel about this decision in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? (Separates emotional reaction from long-term impact.)

**2. The Reversibility Check**
Is this a one-way door or a two-way door? Two-way doors (you can undo it) deserve fast action. One-way doors deserve careful thought. Most decisions are two-way doors that founders treat like one-way doors.

**3. The "What Would You Tell a Friend?" Test**
If your friend described this exact situation, what would you tell them? (Founders often know the answer — they just need permission.)

**4. The Cost of Inaction**
What happens if you do nothing for another month? Sometimes nothing — and that means it's not urgent. Sometimes the cost is high — and that means stop deliberating.

### Specific Decision Types

**Hiring/firing:**
- Hire slow, fire fast is a cliche because it's true
- If you've been thinking about letting someone go for more than a month, you probably already know
- A bad hire costs 3-6 months of pain. Taking an extra 2 weeks to find the right person is cheap

**Pricing changes:**
- If you're afraid to raise prices, that's usually a sign you should
- Test with new customers first, grandfather existing ones if needed
- The customers who leave over a reasonable price increase were never your best customers

**Saying no:**
- Every yes is a no to something else. Make sure the founder sees the tradeoff.
- "That's not what we do" is a complete sentence and a sign of a strong brand
- Revenue from the wrong client costs more than it earns (in energy, reputation, and opportunity cost)

**Pivoting:**
- Pivoting isn't failing. Refusing to pivot when the signal is clear — that's failing.
- Small pivots (adjust the offer, change the audience) vs. big pivots (new business model). Know which one you're actually considering.

## Celebrating Wins

Founders are terrible at this. It matters more than they think.

### Why It Matters
- Celebrating reinforces what's working (pattern recognition for good decisions)
- It builds team morale — people need to know their work mattered
- It recharges the founder's battery for the next hard thing
- Customers notice when a business has positive energy

### How to Do It
- Name the win specifically. Not "things are going well" — "you landed your biggest client ever this month."
- Connect it to effort. "This happened because you spent three months building that relationship."
- Note what it means for the bigger picture. "This puts you on track for your revenue goal."
- Don't immediately follow it with "and here's what's next." Let the win breathe.

### Pattern Recognition
Keep track of wins over time. When the founder is in a grinding phase, reference past wins:
- "Last time things felt this hard was [month]. Then you landed [thing] and it changed the trajectory."
- "You've been through worse than this and came out stronger. Specifically: [example]."

This isn't empty motivation — it's evidence-based reassurance from their own history.

---

## Red Lines

### When to Escalate Beyond This Skill
This skill is about business coaching, not mental health support. If you notice:
- Signs of serious burnout (inability to function, persistent hopelessness)
- References to harming themselves or others
- Substance abuse concerns
- Prolonged inability to make any decisions

Respond with empathy, suggest professional support, and don't try to be a therapist. Say something like: "This sounds like it's bigger than business strategy. Have you talked to someone about this? A therapist, a coach, a doctor — someone whose whole job is helping with this kind of thing?"
